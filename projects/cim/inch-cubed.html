Inch Cubed

CIM - Mr. Uken - Period 1

Project Dates: 8/20/2024 - 9/16/2024

Full Documentation ↗
The Inch Cubed project is, just like its name suggests, based on what you can create out of exactly 1 in3 of 3D print filament. We were tasked with creating a 3D model on a CAD software (I chose Autodesk Fusion 360), and it can be ANYTHING, as long as it:
  • has a volume under 1 inch cubed
  • fits within the build plate, preferably 6in x 6in x 4in

    • I chose to create a laptop stand because I thought it would be the most practical in my day-to day life. I have been wanting to create an angled elevation stand for my laptop for a while, as it would then provide adequate ventilation, which is a need as my laptop frequently overheats. I started off by taking some crucial measurements of my laptop's underside, and calculating the angle and elevation of the laptop with the stand using the Pythagorean Theorem. Then, I used that specific height and width to create a very rough, rectangular elevated/angled model of a laptop stand on Fusion 360.

    I decided to print my project in the horizontal upright position, which made it ideal for strength, as the layers would have to go through compression, not tension, so it would be better if the layers were stacked on top of each other, instead of next to each other. Otherwise, the structural integrity would be a lot less, and the layers would be a lot more likely to split apart. Also, the print came out very accurate, since I didn't have many tiny details. However, there were a couple spots where the print had a few impurities, such as corners/edges which I filleted, and of course the angled section on top was not too smooth.

    From this project, I learned that the type of 3D printing and the filament that we used is pretty sturdy and accurate, unless you are trying to make very precise details smaller than 1/10 inches big. I didn't get such problems in my part. However, I need to make sure to use correct material in model, otherwise mass estimation will be inaccurate, and to be patient to wait for the support material to dissolve.

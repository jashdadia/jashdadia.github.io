Entrepreneurship Project 2026

DE - Ms. Chou - Period 6

Project Dates: 3/11/2026 - 4/1/2026

Full Project Documentation ↗

Our project focused on creating a simple, low‑cost tool to help new drivers learn how to accelerate and brake more smoothly. We started by brainstorming problems we all personally struggled with when learning to drive, and we realized harsh acceleration and braking was something every beginner deals with. As a team, we wrote out the problem together and began exploring possible solutions that could realistically be built with the hardware available in class.

After discussing different ideas, we agreed that using an Arduino, a breadboard, and an accelerometer would give us the most flexibility. I helped shape this direction by contributing to our early planning and documenting our progress. Once we committed to the Arduino approach, I took the lead on building our first working prototype. I wrote most of the Arduino code in C++, with Aarav helping me refine parts of it since he has more CS experience. I also tested the prototype in my own car, collected data from the Serial Monitor, and adjusted the accelerometer sensitivity so the alerts felt accurate and not overly sensitive.

Working with the Arduino and accelerometer taught me how to tune hardware to behave consistently in real‑world conditions. I had to balance sensitivity, noise, and timing so the device only alerted the driver when acceleration or braking was actually unsafe. This made the project feel similar to real driver‑training tools, where reliability matters more than complexity.

Throughout the project, I also helped with documentation and research. I looked into existing products on the market and found that there are almost no tools designed specifically to help new drivers practice smoother driving, which confirmed that our idea filled a real gap. Later, I grabbed a piezo buzzer from class, wired it into our circuit at home, and updated the Arduino code so the buzzer would activate during harsh driving events.

I tested the updated prototype again in a safe environment and recorded a demo showing the buzzer and LEDs responding correctly to sudden acceleration and braking. Near the end of the project, I took notes on other groups’ presentations, helped record our team’s explanation video, and wrote out the full product‑design process since I had the most hands‑on experience with the prototype. Finally, I presented our project to the class with my group.
